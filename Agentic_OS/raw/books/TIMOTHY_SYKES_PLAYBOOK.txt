SECTION 1 -- TRADER PLAYBOOK SUMMARY
========================================

Timothy Sykes is a pattern-recognition long-biased trader (with some short exposure)
who specializes in promoted penny stocks and OTC/small-cap names that follow a
predictable lifecycle from hype to collapse. His core insight is that these stocks
follow the same emotional arc -- hype, euphoria, distribution, panic, bounce -- and
that the patterns are repeatable regardless of the underlying company or sector.

His primary vehicle is the morning panic dip buy on stocks that have had multi-day or
multi-week runs. He claims this single pattern accounts for one-third to one-half of
his total career trading profits, which he documents publicly on Profit.ly.

He trades small. His personal trading account is intentionally kept small (by trader
standards) and all profits are donated to charity. His personal P&L ranges from
$80K-$240K per year in documented recent years. His business income is primarily
from the Trading Challenge education product.

He is primarily a teacher who still trades to demonstrate his system. His trading
volume is low relative to his students'. He explicitly states he misses or undersizes
many setups intentionally because he is focused on demonstrating the process, not
maximizing personal P&L.

Operating principle: "Aim small, miss small." Take small, defined-risk trades on
high-probability patterns. Never try to catch the full move. Preservation over
maximization.

Confirmed current bias: Long-biased. He has explicitly moved away from short selling
in recent years due to short squeeze risk in small-cap names. Quote from 2022-2023:
"I stopped shorting stocks" and "without them [short sellers] we wouldn't have so
many great opportunities." He actively uses overly aggressive short sellers as
evidence of long-side squeeze fuel.

SECTION 3 -- SETUP CATALOG
========================================

--------
SETUP 1: MORNING PANIC DIP BUY (PRIMARY SETUP)
Confidence: High
Sources: timothysykes.com/blog/dip-buy-morning-panics,
         timothysykes.com/blog/perfect-morning-panic,
         timothysykes.com/blog/7-steps-to-mastering-the-panic-dip-buy,
         timothysykes.com/blog/do-this-every-morning
--------

Description:
A stock that has run for several days to several weeks opens and sells off sharply
(30% or more from the prior session close or overnight high). The panic is fast and
mechanical. At some point the selling exhausts and buyers absorb. The stock bounces.

Required context:
Stock must have had a multi-day or multi-week catalyst-driven run prior to the panic.
The bigger and longer the run, the better the bounce quality.
Promoter-backed OTC stocks are his historical sweet spot because promoters have
incentive to create a soft landing (they need price above their cost basis).

Catalyst precondition:
Catalyst must have driven the multi-day run. PR, promotion, social hype, sector theme.
No catalyst run = no predictable panic bounce. This is not a random dip buy.

Entry trigger:
First stabilization after the panic. First green candle or visible momentum stall.
Wait for the sell-off to visibly exhaust before entering. Do not buy mid-fall.
Window: 9:30 AM to 12:00 PM ET only.

Invalidation point:
Panic continues through the entry level. No bounce develops.
Afternoon panic (first red day afternoon) = do not enter. High failure rate.

Stop logic:
Cut quickly if no bounce. He explicitly says: "Panics don't always bounce."
Stop is typically just below the entry / panic low.
He does not specify a fixed % stop. Structural stop only.

Target logic:
Quick 10-20% or 30-50% bounce. Goal is to collect a defined percentage, not ride
the full recovery. He describes taking 1/4 to 1/3 of the potential move as his
typical capture due to conservative sizing.
"I'm just trying to take advantage of a short-term move."

Common failure mode:
Averaging down when the bounce does not materialize immediately.
Holding too long and giving back gains when the stock continues to fade.
Entering a slow afternoon panic rather than a sharp morning panic.

Confidence: High. Confirmed as his stated primary edge and repeatedly demonstrated.


--------
SETUP 2: THE 7-STEP SUPERNOVA -- LONG-SIDE STAGES 2-4 ENTRY
Confidence: High
Sources: timothysykes.com/blog/7-step-pennystocking-framework,
         timothysykes.com/blog/gtii-my-7-step-framework,
         timothysykes.com/blog/7-step-framework-predicted-this-weekend-trade
--------

Description:
The 7-step pennystocking framework maps the full lifecycle of a promoted/hyped penny
stock. The seven stages are:

Stage 1: Initial spike / first discovery (low volume, first catalyst)
Stage 2: Pullback / consolidation after initial spike
Stage 3: Second leg up / breakout above first spike high
Stage 4: Peak / climax / supernova exhaustion (most dramatic move)
Stage 5: First selloff / major panic
Stage 6: Dead cat bounce / recovery attempt
Stage 7: Final fade to lows / stock dies

Long entries: Stages 2-3 (early re-entry on pullback before second leg).
             Stage 5-6 (dip buy the panic, sell into the dead cat bounce).
Short entries: Stage 4 top (short the supernova peak) -- now largely avoided.
              Stage 6-7 transition (short the dead cat bounce failure).

His personal preference: Stages 5-6 for long-side dip buys.
Quote: "I find trading between steps 5 and 6 to be more consistent for most traders."

Required context:
Stock must be identifiably in one of the framework stages. Not every stock qualifies.
Must have had a real catalyst-driven spike as the foundation for the cycle.

Entry trigger:
Stage 2: Pullback to support after initial spike with volume drying up.
Stage 5-6: First panic low with volume exhaustion, entering the dead cat bounce.

Stop: Just below the stage entry level. Cut quickly if stage progression fails.

Target:
Stage 2 entry: Next leg toward stage 3 high.
Stage 5-6 entry: The dead cat bounce recovery. Do not hold through stage 7.

Failure mode:
Misidentifying which stage the stock is actually in.
Holding a stage 5-6 bounce too long and riding it into stage 7 (final collapse).


--------
SETUP 3: FIRST GREEN DAY (FGD) LONG
Confidence: High
Sources: stockstotrade.com/first-green-day-pattern,
         timothysykes.com/blog/7-steps-to-mastering-the-panic-dip-buy,
         multiple trade recaps on Profit.ly
--------

Description:
A stock that has been in a downtrend or base suddenly has a high-volume green day
driven by a fresh catalyst. The first green day is the entry signal for the next
potential multi-day move.

Required context:
Fresh, confirmed catalyst from the current session or recent session.
High relative volume vs recent average. Volume confirms the catalyst is real.
Stock was previously fading or base-building, not already extended.
For OTC stocks: catalyst spreading slowly = more time to enter. Listed stocks:
catalyst spreads faster, afternoon entry preferred.

Entry trigger:
For OTC: Buy on or shortly after the first green day, as the catalyst is spreading.
For listed stocks: Afternoon confirmation that the move is holding.
He waits to see if early buyers hold their positions before committing.

Stop: Below the first green day low. If it gives back the catalyst, exit.

Target: Second and third green day continuation.

Common failure mode:
Entering too late after the first green day has already moved 50%+.
No follow-through on day 2 if volume does not confirm.


--------
SETUP 4: BUYABLE PULLBACK IN STRONG UPTREND (RUNAWAY STOCK ENTRY)
Confidence: Medium
Sources: timothysykes.com/blog/the-only-way-to-buy-into-strength
--------

Description:
A stock moving fast with a strong news-driven catalyst that Sykes does not want to
chase outright. He waits for a brief intraday pullback to a short-term support level,
then enters with the momentum to the upside.

Required context:
Extremely strong catalyst with real potential (not vague PR). His example was EMGE.
Stock must be proven already moving. Not anticipatory.
He explicitly states this is NOT his normal mode. He adapted to it for special cases.
"Normally, I don't like to buy into strength."

Entry: Quick dip within the uptrend. Buy into the dip, not at the high.
Stop: Below the dip low. If the pullback continues, exit.
Target: Continuation of the original momentum move.

Confidence: Medium. He teaches it but describes it as a situational adaptation.


--------
SETUP 5: AFTERNOON DOUBLE-BOTTOM PANIC DIP BUY (NUANCE/EXCEPTION)
Confidence: Medium
Sources: timothysykes.com/blog/penny-stock-nuances
--------

Description:
A stock panics in the afternoon (which he normally avoids). It attempts to make a
low, fails, then probes a second low shortly thereafter. The second bottom is a
potential entry -- better odds than the first bottom.

Required context:
Must be a double-bottom structure (two bottom attempts, not a one-time flush).
Late in the day = lower probability. Only applicable under specific conditions.
He explicitly warns: "The later in the day, the less likely this setup will work."

Entry: After the second bottom forms and shows stabilization.
Stop: Below the second bottom low.
Target: Quick intraday recovery, 10-20%.

Confidence: Medium. He teaches it as an exception to his normal no-afternoon rule.

SECTION 4 -- PATTERN AND CONTEXT PREFERENCES
========================================

Chart structures he repeats most:
Multi-day spike into panic: his primary pattern. Stock runs for days or weeks then
opens with a sharp sell-off. He buys the bounce.

Volume signatures that matter to him:
Volume spike on panic = seller exhaustion possible. Watch for stabilization.
Volume dry-up on pullback/base = accumulation potential, next leg likely.
No volume on fake bounce = don't chase, bounce likely to fail.

He explicitly uses volume at support and resistance levels to confirm their strength:
"General areas where the price hovers for 30 minutes to an hour are the strongest."

Momentum conditions:
He does not require momentum to be present at entry. He buys into the panic bottom,
which by definition has no momentum. He then sells into the recovering momentum.
This is a mean-reversion/bounce approach, not a pure momentum chase.

Regime preferences:
Hot market with active sector themes = deploy more aggressively.
Slow/quiet market = reduce activity or stop trading entirely.
He describes taking weeks or months off when there are no quality setups.
Quote: "I wait for the big moves. I'm not trying to make money every day."

Catalyst hierarchy:
Tier 1 (best): Confirmed earnings beat, FDA approval, real revenue news.
Tier 2: Sector theme news, partnership announcements with substance.
Tier 3: Vague PR, promotional fluff, LOI, MOU = lower confidence.
He explicitly warns against vague PRs but acknowledges that promoted stocks with
weak catalysts still follow the supernova pattern.

Promoter-backed stocks:
He still trades stocks he knows are likely promotions because the pattern is
predictable. "I don't dip buy offerings" is his primary filter for promoter stocks.
He avoids stocks that are diluting via offerings because the soft landing

SECTION 5 -- EXECUTION FRAMEWORK
========================================

Entry style:
Conservative. He describes himself as getting in too conservatively and capturing
only 1/4 to 1/3 of the potential move on most dip buys.
Does not attempt to catch the exact bottom. Waits for evidence of stabilization.
Limit orders implied by his precision-focused language. Does not chase.

Confirmation requirements:
First stabilization candle after panic. Volume slowing. Price not continuing lower.
Does not enter until the panic velocity visibly slows.

Aggressiveness vs patience:
Patience is explicitly the core discipline. He describes waiting for the pattern to
be "perfect" before acting. Most days he does not trade at all.
"Not every panic is the same, but the overall pattern hasn't changed."

Adding strategy:
No described consistent adding-to-winner strategy. His trades are typically entered
in one tranche and exited quickly. He does not describe pyramiding.

Trimming strategy:
Sells quickly into the bounce recovery. Takes 10-20% often, leaving the rest on the
table. Deliberately under-captures to ensure he is always selling, not holding.
"It's vital to sell into excessive strength/hype, do not just hold and hope."

Exit logic:
Bounce recovery = partial to full exit.
If the bounce fails to develop or price fades = cut quickly.
He never holds overnight if the thesis is based on a morning panic bounce.
Swing holds only if the multi-day framework clearly supports it.

When he holds vs cuts:
Holds: When the 7-step stage analysis supports multi-day continuation.
       When the catalyst is strong and confirmed.
Cuts: When price action fails to confirm the bounce.
      When the stock is entering a probable stage 7 (final collapse).
      Any time he was wrong about the stage identification.

Signs of low-quality trade:
Afternoon panic (not morning).
No prior multi-day run to establish the emotional baseline.
Vague catalyst without real substance.
Stock in stage 7 (no dead cat bounce expected).
Dilution via active offering ongoing (no promoter incentive for soft landing).
Spread too wide for clean exit.

Students observing his personal trade results may be misled about the ceiling.

CONTRADICTION 3: "Not trying to make money every day" vs daily alerts and engagement
He explicitly says he waits for quality setups and doesn't trade daily.
Yet his platform requires daily engagement (alerts, webinars, chat room presence).
This business model pressure likely creates tension between his stated discipline
and his commercial activity. Whether he trades out of conviction vs platform
obligation is not documentable from public sources.
Confidence: Medium.

CONTRADICTION 4: 1:3 or 1:4 R:R target vs described trade captures
He states 1:3 or 1:4 R:R minimum on the Sykes Sliding Scale.
Yet he describes typically capturing 1/4 to 1/3 of the potential move on panics,
which implies the actual realized R:R on many trades is much lower.
Possible reconciliation: the 1:3 ratio is the potential before entry, not the
realized capture. He intentionally exits early.
Confidence: Medium tension here.

CONTRADICTION 5: Avoids promoted stocks with dilution vs trades promoted stocks
He says "I don't dip buy offerings" (active dilution = no soft landing possible).
Yet many of the OTC penny stocks he trades are promotions with known dilution risk.
His filter is whether the dilution is actively being executed at the time of the
trade, not whether the company has a history of dilution.
This is a nuance, not a clear contradiction, but it creates confusion for students.

SECTION 9 -- REAL EDGE ASSESSMENT
========================================

LIKELY REAL EDGE:

1. Pattern frequency in promoted/hyped penny stocks.
Promoted stocks follow a highly predictable lifecycle because the promoters create it
deliberately. The supernova arc (hype, spike, panic, bounce, collapse) is engineered
by the promoters and is therefore more reliable than random market patterns.
Sykes recognized this structural predictability early and built his framework around it.
His edge is in reading a manufactured cycle, not a natural market cycle.
This is a genuine structural edge that most traders do not recognize.

2. Morning panic dip buy on high-liquidity multi-week runners.
When a stock has run for weeks, the emotional base of trapped longs and panicking
sellers is large. The morning panic on these names produces a predictable bounce
because the same mechanics repeat: forced selling, stop cascades, then stabilization.
Timing the stabilization with a tight structural stop creates a favorable entry.

3. Pattern library and preparation quality.
His teaching method is built on memorizing hundreds of named patterns until recognition
is automatic. The competitive edge is speed of identification and preparedness.
A trader who has internalized 800+ dip buy video lessons will recognize the setup
faster than one who has not. Speed of recognition = ability to enter near the bottom.

4. Discipline to only trade when the pattern is present.
Most retail traders overtrade. His willingness to not trade on dull days reduces
unnecessary losses that erode edge. Selectivity is documented and confirmed.

WHAT APPEARS TO BE BRANDING, NOT EDGE:

1. The $12,415 to $2M bar mitzvah money story.
This was his 2000-2001 result in one of the most speculative periods in US market
history. Repeating this in 2025 is marketing context, not strategy context.

2. "Over 30 millionaire students" as proof of concept.
The selection bias is severe. Thousands of students, a handful of high performers.
The strategy may work for skilled practitioners but the hit rate among typical
students is not reported with precision.

3. The teaching-charity-travel lifestyle positioning.
His personal brand is largely about freedom and lifestyle. This is marketing.
His actual trading P&L ($80K-$240K/year recently) is modest for a claimed strategy.

4. Supernova terminology and branding.
The term supernova is his coinage and is now widely used. The pattern itself
(parabolic spike then collapse) predates him and exists across markets.
His brand has been attached to a universal phenomenon, not a proprietary discovery.

SECTION 10 -- RULES-BASED RECONSTRUCTION
========================================

SETUP CRITERIA:
Stock has had a multi-day or multi-week catalyst-driven run.
The run has a confirmed underlying catalyst (not fully vague PR).
Stock is showing sharp morning sell-off (30%+) in the first 30-60 minutes.
Sell-off is fast and mechanical, not slow grind.
Stock has a history of bouncing from panics (check prior chart behavior).
Active dilution offering is NOT in progress at time of entry.
Time is between 9:30 AM and 12:00 PM ET.
Spread is acceptable for clean entry and exit.

ENTRY CHECKLIST:
Identify which 7-step stage the stock is in. Confirm stage 5 or close to stage 6.
Wait for the panic to visibly stall. Do not buy mid-fall.
First stabilization candle or first green candle confirms.
Apply Sykes Sliding Scale PREPARE acronym before entry:
  P - Pattern identified clearly
  R - Risk/reward at least 1:3 potential
  E - Ease of entry/exit confirmed (liquidity check)
  P - Past history of spiking and bouncing confirmed
  A - Time window confirmed (9:30 AM - 12:00 PM ET)
  R - Real catalyst driving the original run
  E - Environment (hot market, active sector theme if possible)
Enter with small initial position.

RISK CHECKLIST:
Stop defined before entry: just below the panic low.
Liquidity confirmed: position size manageable relative to average daily volume.
Active offering? If yes, do not enter (no soft landing incentive).
Afternoon panic? If yes, skip or reduce size drastically.
Is this stage 7 (final collapse)? If yes, do not enter long.

EXIT CHECKLIST:
Bounce of 10-20% from entry = consider first partial exit.
Do not hold through parabolic extension looking for stage 4 again.
If price fades back through entry within 30 minutes = exit 100%. Thesis failed.
Never hold overnight a morning panic dip buy unless multi-day framework supports it.
Take 1/4 to 1/3 of potential move, not the full potential.
"Sell into excessive strength/hype."

NO-TRADE CONDITIONS:
Stock is panicking in the afternoon (past 12:00 PM ET).
Active dilution offering in progress.
No prior multi-day run establishing the emotional base.
Catalyst was vague, promotional, or has already been fully discounted.
Stock is in stage 7 of the 7-step framework (final collapse, no bounce expected).
Spread too wide for clean entry and exit.
Market environment is slow/quiet with no active themes.
The pattern is not clearly identifiable from the chart.
You feel the urge to trade but cannot identify which stage the stock is in.

DAILY PROCESS FRAMEWORK:
Pre-market (before 9:30 AM):
  Scan for overnight gappers and prior day spikers.
  Build three-tier watchlist: prior day spikers, pre-market gappers, multi-day runners.
  Identify which tier-3 names (multi-day runners) are potential panic dip buy targets.
  Apply Sykes Sliding Scale to each candidate. Grade pre-market, not intraday.

Market open (9:30 AM - 12:00 PM):
  Watch tier-3 candidates for morning panic patterns.
  Do not trade the spike itself. Wait for the panic.
  Execute dip buy on first stabilization if all criteria are met.
  Exit quickly into bounce recovery. Do not overstay.

Midday to close:
  Review trades. Log results on Profit.ly or equivalent.
  If no quality morning setups, do not force afternoon trades.
  Look for first green day setups developing in sector theme names.

Post-market:
  Study one trade video lesson or chart pattern daily.
  Review watchlist for next day. Which multi-day runners are still intact?
