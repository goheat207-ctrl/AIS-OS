Candle Body Size


The size of the candle body communicates sentiment. The larger the candle body, the stronger the sentiment.


What Candle Wicks Tell Us


Candle wicks communicate a battle.

Upper candle wicks are bearish, because they show that sellers were stronger and pulled the price back down.

Lower candle wicks are bullish, because they show buyers were stronger and pulled the price back up.

A Long Body Candle shows extreme strength in the market. The price opens at the bottom of the candle, then surges and closes at the top of the candle. This is a very bullish indicator.
This is the same with long body red candles. They are a sign of extreme weakness.





Long Body Candles






A Short Body Candle communicates less strength than a Long Body Candle. Typically when the price is moving up, we want to see the candles getting longer and longer, showing increasing strength. If the opposite is true, the candles are getting shorter and shorter, it may mean the trend is becoming exhausted.






Doji Candles


In order for a Doji candle to be created, the price must open, then fall, then rise, then close back at more or less the same price.

Doji candles are considered candles of indecision.

Indecision after a strong trend indicates a possible reversal. Therefore we pay close attention to Doji Candles at the top or bottom of a long series of trending candles.

Horizontal Support/Resistance Lines
Horizontal Support/Resistance Lines are drawn on any time frame, from 1min, 5min, to the daily chart, based on what we perceive as being resistance or support points.
Often we will draw a horizontal line at the top of a recent high, or the bottom of a recent low.
Support and Resistance lines are more valid when they have been tested multiple times without being broken.

















Resistance at 1/2 and Whole Dollars
Many traders will put orders to take pro?t at logical places such at $5.00, or $5.50.

As a result, there is often a signi?cant amount of resistance/support associated with 1/2 dollars and whole dollars. These form horizontal support/resistance lines on a chart.
It's very common once a level breaks (support or resistance), that price will retest it con?rm it can hold. If it holds, that changes from support to resistance.

Ascending/Descending Support & Resistance Lines
Ascending & Descending Support or Resistance Lines are drawn by connecting recent highs or lows in a way where the line is touched multiple times by price action.
If a line only touches in 2 places (the place it started and the place it ends) it hasn't been tested and it's validity is unknown.
Ascending & Descending Support/Resistance lines should be obvious. When they are obvious to you, they are obvious to thousands of other traders as well.







These are valid both on Daily Charts and Intraday Charts

How to Draw a Trend Line (Correctly)


In addition to the support, resistance, and triggers formed by candlestick highs and lows, we also ?nd support and resistance from trend lines.
Trend lines can be ascending or descending lines that connect several candles.

A trend line gains validity the MORE times it's been tapped. A trend line does not need to connect at a speci?c place on a candle to be valid. It can connect to the highs, at the top of the body, or a speci?c price.
Just like a ?at top breakout, the more times we tap, the more validity the resistance level has.

If your trendline only has 2 taps, it might not be very strong.

Some trendlines are so valid, they provide support/resistance for years.

Reading Charts from Right to Left
Although it's counter intuitive, we read charts right to left in the sense that the most recent price action is the most important. In fact, it's so important, it can negate the technical levels of older price action.


For instance, if 3 months ago a stock had resistance around $10.00, but is now trading at between $12-14, the resistance at $10 is no longer signi?cant. We would instead focus on the recent resistance around $14.


I look from Right to Left to look for nearby support or resistance levels



Rules of Charting
1. We read from most recent to historical, from Right to Left, looking for nearby support/resistance.

2. You cannot "skip" over candles. A large candle will "block" any price action that came before it. "Shadow Theory"

3. We always look for obvious patterns, and obvious support and resistance levels.

4. We always look at price action around psychological areas of resistance/support (whole dollars).

5. We look for Gaps and Windows. A Gap or Window should be "large". That means it's at least 2x the size of an average candlestick, or the Average True Range on the daily chart.
6. Gaps can only be formed when a stock opens higher or lower than it closed the previous day.

7. Windows can be another word for Gaps, OR can be the distance between the top and bottom of a very large candle.





















Windows or Pockets on a Daily Chart
A long body candle on a daily chart that has not yet been broken by more recent price action can form a "window" or a "pocket" where the stock will have little to no resistance until it reaches the top/bottom of that candle.

When I read daily charts looking from Right to Left, I look for large windows where there will be little resistance because this will often result in cleaner price action versus a stock chart that has a lot of nearby resistance levels.

Some people will call these gaps, however that is not the correct term because the area was not created by a gapping candle, it was created by a long body candle.
A window should be visually larger than the average candle size in order to be signi?cant.

Windows on Daily Charts in the form of large candles





Gap Fill on a Daily Chart
When a large gap is created on a daily chart, in the future, if the stock gets back into the area of the gap, the next level of horizontal resistance in the form of price action will be once the gap is completely closed. This can offer a large amount of space for potential price action.
It's important to be aware that others areas of resistance formed by moving averages or ascending/descending support resistance lines can be present.
A gap should be visually larger than the average candle size in order to be signi?cant.

Which Time Frame Should I Use?



You can pull up any time frame and see candlestick chart patterns. Active day traders end to rely on "faster" or "shorter" time frames such as 1-min and 5-min charts.
Traders who hold longer my instead rely on 15-minute or 60-minute or even 240-minute charts, in addition to standard daily charts.


Searching for Multi Time Frame Alignment



If we see a bull ?ag on a 5-min chart, AND a 1-min chart, we would call that multi time frame alignment.
Multi-time frame alignment is ideal. I will rarely take a 5-min setup if the 1-min pattern doesn't also offer a low risk setup, although I will occasionally take a 1-min setup when the 5-min setup isn't lined up perfectly.
This will become more clear as you keep learning!


Circumstances when I would NOT use the 1min chart



1. If the stock is extremely extended already on the 5min chart.

2. If the stock has already experienced more than 2 pullbacks on the 1min chart since the breakout began. (In other words, it's most likely extended on the 5min chart and needs to pullback and give us a 5min bull ?ag or ?at top.)
3. I'm less likely to rely on the 1min chart on higher priced stocks as they can be very choppy with lots of fake outs.

4. If the 1min chart is extremely extended off the 20ema, I'll be more cautious, even though I might still take a trade.

5. I rarely take a 1min entry if the 1min price is below the 1min 20EMA.


Circumstances when I WOULD use the 1min chart



Circumstances where I'm very likely to use the 1min chart even if the 5min chart doesn't show a pattern yet.

1. When a stock that meets all of our criteria for being a big potential stock (low ?oat, huge volume spike, great daily chart, etc) has just hit my HOD scanner, I'll look for ?rst 1min pullback, even if it's a micro pullback.

1. Moving Averages

Moving Averages are averaging the price over a set period of time.

Moving Average prices will vary between time frames because the average price of a stock over nine 5-min candles is different from nine daily candles.



Simple Moving Averages vs Exponential Moving Averages
There are 2 types of Moving Averages

1. Simple Moving Averages are simply the average price over a set period of time.

2. Exponential Moving Averages take the average price over a set period of time and weigh more recent price action as being more signi?cant. Therefore EMA's move quickly when price moves.
