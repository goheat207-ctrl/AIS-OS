Borrowing Fees are charged per day. Once you've borrowed shares you can trade that block of shares (buying and selling) as many times as you'd like during the course of that day. Covering

To close a short position a trader must "cover" their position. This is buying the stock to cover the shares they borrowed from their broker. Like a long-sided trader, they can scale out of the short position in small increments.


Days to Cover

Some platforms show the "Days to Cover" for a stock. This is a calculation of the total number of shares held short and the number of days it would take for all the shares to cover their position based on current daily volume.
A stock that would take weeks for all shorts to cover shares can become a target for a short squeeze.


Short Squeeze

This is when a stock suddenly starts moving up, and traders who are holding short positions start buying to cover their position, or their broker covers their position for them because they've hit a max loss on their account. This creates an extreme buy/sell imbalance and can lead stocks to making 50-100% moves intraday.

Short Interest

Short Interest is the measurement of the number of shares held short against a company versus the total number of shares available to trade.
In the case of GameStop, short interested exceeded 100%. Although it shouldn't be possible, it is due to the way borrowing and lending works in the market.


High Short Interest

Stocks with high short interest can become targets for a sudden short squeeze especially if the company releases breaking news that is positive. Long biased traders will buy up shares, the prices move higher, and shorts are forced to cover.

4. An Imbalance Between Supply and Demand

A home run is when an investment vehicle moves 20-30% or more, in 1 day.

What creates big moves like this? An incredible amount of demand, combined with a limited amount of supply.
In order words, an imbalance between supply and demand.



Supply Side: Float
It's important to remember that we're trading in a closed market. This means when we buy shares, we're always buying from a seller, and when we sell shares, we're always selling to a buyer.
When a company has its Initial Public Offering, they release a ?xed number of shares onto the open market. This becomes the company's "?oat". The ?oat is simply the number of shares available to trade.

5. Retail Traders vs. Institutional Traders
Institutional traders are licensed by the SEC and work for banks, hedge funds, pension funds, etc. Institutional traders will take positions based on orders from managers. Their mission is to accumulate positions at the best price possible. They create buying support as they accumulate positions when stocks pullback.
Other types of institutional traders utilize High-Frequency Trading (HFT) algos to trade the markets or act as market makers by rapidly buying and selling shares.
